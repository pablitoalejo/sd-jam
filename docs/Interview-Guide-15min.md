# Interview Guide: Communication in Design Jams — 15-Minute Version

**Project:** sd-jam — Service Design JAM Session
**Research Focus:** How participants communicate and where meaning breaks down in design workshops
**Framework:** Jobs-to-Be-Done (JTBD)
**Format:** Time-boxed field interviews, 15 minutes per participant
**Derived from:** `docs/Interview-Guide.md` (full 20-question, 45–60 min version)

---

## Selection Rationale

The full guide has 20 questions across 6 themes. For a 15-minute interview, the goal is not to cover every theme — it is to ask questions broad enough that answers naturally traverse multiple themes without prompting.

The five core questions below were selected on three criteria:

1. **Gateway potential** — does the question's frame naturally pull answers from adjacent themes? A question about arriving at a workshop cold (Q1) will surface context-setting, time pressure, and communication style without separate questions for each.
2. **Story-eliciting over opinion-eliciting** — "tell me about a time" yields richer, more specific data than "what do you think about." Every question here asks for a specific memory.
3. **Front-loaded by importance** — the questions that matter most come first. If the interview runs to 10 minutes instead of 15, you have already collected the highest-value data.

**What was cut and why:**

| Cut | Reason |
|-----|--------|
| Q2, Q3 (effective onboarding, what you wish you knew) | Probes of Q1 — the gateway question absorbs them |
| Q4, Q5 (time pressure) | Time pressure surfaces naturally inside Q1 and Q6 answers; a standalone question is redundant |
| Q9 (losing track of the problem) | Sub-probe of Q8; not worth its own slot |
| Q10, Q11, Q12 (tool/artifact integration) | Important but instrumental; one follow-up probe in Q7 captures it without consuming a full question slot |
| Q13, Q14 (shared frameworks, jargon navigation) | Q6 and Q15 cover common language more precisely and with less overlap |
| Q16, Q17, Q18, Q19 (diverse styles) | Four questions on one theme is disproportionate; merged into a single story-eliciting question (Q5 below) |

## Facilitator Introduction Script (read aloud — 60 seconds)

"Thanks for making time. I want to hear about your real experience in design workshops — specifically where communication works and where it breaks down. No right answers. I'm after specific moments and stories, not general opinions. We have about 15 minutes, so I'll move us through fairly quickly. Ready?"

| 1 | Onboarding + Context | 3 min | Tell me about a time you arrived at a workshop feeling like you were starting from zero — you didn't know the people, the problem, or what was expected. | 2 | Meaning-Making + Common Language | 3 min | Tell me about a time two people in a workshop were using the same word but clearly meant different things. | 3 | Meaning-Making + Tools | 3 min | When you need to explain a complex idea in a workshop — something that's hard to put into words — what do you reach for? What works, and what falls flat? | - Do visual methods land differently than verbal? When? - What do you do when you sense the room isn't following you? - How do the tools available (physical or digital) help or get in the way? - Have you seen AI-generated content enter a workshop? How did the group receive it? | "Communication is also beyond language. It is emotion, culture, people's past experiences, and their future aspirations. We have visual thinkers, auditory thinkers, and kinesthetic aesthetic learners." — Abhishek | Help me express ideas in a form that resonates across different kinds of thinkers, so my contribution lands with the whole room — not just the people who think the way I do. |
| 4 | Alignment + Common Language | 3 min | How do you personally tell the difference between a group that has genuinely aligned and one that has just agreed to move on? Can you give me an example of each? | - What's the tell that alignment is real versus performed? - What does fake alignment feel like in the room? - What creates it, and why does the group allow it? | "Getting people to align on what to focus on, how to communicate in a way where meaning, context, and understanding are all the same, is extremely difficult to do." — Pablo | Help me distinguish genuine alignment from polite agreement so the work we commit to at the end of a session is built on something real and won't unravel in the next phase. |
| 5 | Diverse Communication Styles | 2 min | Think about someone in a workshop who communicated very differently from you — different style, different pace, maybe a different cultural background. How did that affect the collaboration, and what created the conditions for their perspective to actually land? | - Were there moments of friction? How were they handled? - Was there a moment their difference actually helped the group? - What did the facilitator do, or what could they have done? | "Communication is also beyond language. It is emotion, culture, people's past experiences, and their future aspirations. Communication can be different and mean different things to different people." — Abhishek | Help me engage with people who think and communicate differently from me so I'm not inadvertently filtering out perspectives that don't match my default style. |
| C | Closing + Synthesis | 1 min | If you could redesign the opening 20 minutes of any design jam from scratch — knowing everything you've just told me — what would you add, remove, or change? | - How would you know it worked? | "Are we not asking questions up front that could help us make meaning around the topic, around the participants, and around their perspective?" — Claudia | Help me redesign the entry point into collaborative work so the group arrives at productive collaboration faster and with a stronger shared foundation. |

## Time Map

```
0:00 – 0:01   Warm-up (W)         Role + experience calibration
0:01 – 0:04   Question 1          Onboarding story — gateway question, richest yield
0:04 – 0:07   Question 2          Same word / different meaning
0:07 – 0:10   Question 3          Expressing complex ideas + tools
0:10 – 0:13   Question 4          Real vs. surface alignment
0:13 – 0:15   Question 5 + Close  Diverse styles → redesign the opening
```

## Facilitator Timing Notes

**If you are running short** — prioritize in this order:

1. Q1 must run. It is the broadest gateway question and will surface onboarding, time pressure, and context-setting without separate questions.
2. Q2 is the second priority. The "same word, different meaning" frame is the most productive single lens into meaning-making and common language.
3. Q4 (real vs. surface alignment) is the third priority. It surfaces the sharpest insight about what actually goes wrong at the end of a session.
4. Q3 and Q5 can be cut if needed, or collapsed into follow-up probes inside Q1 and Q2.
5. The closing question (C) is valuable but optional — skip it if you are out of time.

**If the participant goes deep on Q1** — do not interrupt. A rich story on Q1 will often answer Q2 and Q5 without you asking. Track the themes covered and skip the corresponding questions.

**If an answer is abstract** — probe with: "Can you give me a specific moment?" or "Tell me the story of one time that happened."

**Watch for signal moments** — hesitation, frustration, or energy spikes in the voice. Those are the places to pause and probe: "Say more about that."

---

## After the Interview (do this immediately)

- Write 3–5 sentences summarising the most vivid story or insight before you move to the next participant.
- Tag each response with theme labels: Onboarding, Meaning-Making, Tools, Common Language, Diverse Styles.
- Flag any quotes that are vivid enough to carry into an affinity map or research readout.
