# Interview Guide: Communication in Design Jam Workshops

**Project:** sd-jam — Service Design JAM Session
**Research Focus:** How participants communicate and what challenges they face during design jam workshops
**Framework:** Jobs-to-Be-Done (JTBD)
**Audience:** Design jam participants, facilitators, workshop practitioners

---

## Facilitator Introduction Script

> Read this aloud at the start of each interview. Keep a conversational tone — this is not a formal debrief.

---

"Thank you for taking the time to speak with me today. I want to learn about your real experience in design jam workshops — specifically around how you communicate with others, how the group makes meaning together, and where things get hard.

There are no right or wrong answers. I'm not testing your knowledge. I'm trying to understand your actual lived experience — the good, the frustrating, and everything in between.

I may ask you to recall specific moments or tell me a story. That's intentional. Concrete examples are far more useful to us than general opinions.

I'll be taking notes and, with your permission, recording the session so I can focus on listening. Do I have your consent to record?

We have about 45 to 60 minutes. Feel free to take your time, redirect me if something doesn't feel right, and ask me to clarify any question.

Let's start with a bit of context about you."

---

### Warm-Up (5 minutes)

Ask these before diving into the main questions. They lower the guard and establish rapport.

1. How long have you been participating in or facilitating design workshops?
2. What kind of workshop formats have you worked in — remote, in-person, hybrid?
3. What draws you to this type of collaborative work?

## Closing Script

> Read this aloud at the end of each interview.

---

"We're wrapping up now. Before I let you go, a few final questions:

1. Is there anything about communication in design workshops that I didn't ask about, that you think is important?
2. Is there a moment or story from a past workshop that came to mind during our conversation that you didn't get a chance to share?
3. Is there anyone you'd recommend I speak with — someone who has a very different perspective or experience from yours?

Thank you. This has been genuinely useful. The patterns and stories you've shared will directly shape how we approach this problem. I'll follow up if I have any clarifying questions, and I'll share findings with the group once we've completed all the interviews."

## Facilitator Notes

**Before the interview:**
- Review the participant's background if known. Flag which themes are most relevant to their role.
- Print or have this guide open but resist reading from it robotically. The table is a reference, not a script.

**During the interview:**
- Prioritize stories over opinions. If an answer is abstract, probe with "Can you give me a specific example?"
- If a participant volunteers a story that maps to a later question, note it and skip the later question to avoid repetition.
- Watch for emotional language — hesitation, frustration, energy. Those are signal moments to probe deeper.

**After the interview:**
- Write a 3–5 sentence summary immediately after the session while memory is fresh.
- Tag each response with theme labels (Onboarding, Meaning-Making, Tools, Common Language, Diverse Styles) to facilitate synthesis across participants.
- Note any quotes that are vivid enough to use in a research readout or affinity map.
