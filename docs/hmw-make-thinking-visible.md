# How Might We Make Thinking Visible?

20 variations grounded in our research synthesis. Each statement contains the core phrase "make thinking visible."

---

1. **How might we make thinking visible through shared artifacts during term-definition moments** so that mixed-experience workshop participants can recognize semantic divergence before it silently derails a decision?
*Grounded in Bianca's discovery that "alignment" meant consensus to some and compromise to others — a gap that only surfaced after friction had already accumulated.*

2. **How might we make their thinking visible in writing before any voice is heard aloud** so that introverted or junior participants can contribute ideas that get weighed on merit rather than volume?
*Grounded in Kevin's and Sergio's repeated use of silent sticky-note brainstorming specifically to prevent the loudest person from setting the direction.*

3. **How might we make collective thinking visible through a living vocabulary wall** so that first-time Service Jam participants can orient themselves to workshop language without stopping the room to ask?
*Grounded in Preston's need for context before sessions and Amber's practice of explicit level-setting at Marriott as a non-negotiable first step.*

4. **How might we make thinking visible at the moment an aha insight occurs** so that workshop participants can capture emergent realizations in real time rather than losing them before the designated debrief?
*Grounded in Brandon's pain point: "the idea dies as soon as you leave it — why don't you capture it as it's happening?"*

5. **How might we make their thinking visible through visual externalization of assumptions** so that facilitators working in hierarchical organizations can expose false consensus before the group checks the box and walks away misaligned?
*Grounded in Sergio's description of alignment theater: "we walk away thinking we agree, and nobody has really agreed — then you're in swirl."*

6. **How might we make thinking visible by translating complex ideas into domain-specific analogies** so that non-design stakeholders in workshops can engage with a concept without first needing to decode the jargon used to describe it?
*Grounded in Yosef's bridging technique of asking "tell me what you do" and meeting people in their own field, and Jeffery's elevator-being-built-while-occupied analogy.*

7. **How might we make our thinking visible across isolated breakout groups** so that a pollinator-style participant can carry live insights between teams and prevent silos from forming during a multi-group workshop session?
*Grounded in Brandon's description of the deliberate pollinator role at civic hackathons: "constantly making sure we didn't get siloed — build bridges with questions."*

8. **How might we make thinking visible through a shared surface that all participants author simultaneously** so that quieter contributors in a workshop are not dependent on a facilitator to extract and represent their perspective?
*Grounded in Brandon's contrast between participatory coherence (1-2-4-All) and "someone extracting an insight" — where the latter systematically underweights quieter voices.*

9. **How might we make their thinking visible at the onboarding stage** so that experienced jammers and first-timers entering the same session can calibrate to each other's starting points before the work begins?
*Grounded in Brandon's observation that "not everybody was starting with the same parameters" and Yosef's point that the jam means something fundamentally different depending on how many times you've done it.*

10. **How might we make thinking visible through real-time annotated sketches** so that cognitively depleted participants who arrive at partial capacity can re-engage with a concept without having to reconstruct what they missed verbally?
*Grounded in Brandon's explicit framing of cognitive overload as a design constraint: "we're dealing with people at partial capacity — how do you be generous and gentle in that regard?"*

11. **How might we make our thinking visible by externalizing implicit group norms alongside stated ground rules** so that facilitators can intervene on the actual behavioral contract in the room, not just the one they declared?
*Grounded in Sergio's example of a Slack thread mocking a participant after the meeting — an implicit norm that silenced voices in every subsequent session.*

12. **How might we make thinking visible at the moment two people are talking past each other** so that a facilitator can surface each person's actual meaning and get the group unstuck before the conflict becomes load-bearing?
*Grounded in Brandon's echoing and mirroring technique, described as a way to "trace the conversational memory" and create a pause for reorientation.*

13. **How might we make their thinking visible through a structured silent ideation phase** so that non-native speakers and newcomers in a workshop can contribute fully-formed ideas before the verbal conversation establishes a dominant frame?
*Grounded in the cross-participant signal on voice equity (6 of 9) and Sergio's point that dominance replicates "whatever the two people in the room that dominated the conversation were saying."*

14. **How might we make collective thinking visible by mapping decision points in real time** so that workshop participants can distinguish which conclusions represent genuine shared understanding and which ones are surface compliance driven by seniority?
*Grounded in Sergio's framing of the VP dynamic: "they're agreeing because the VP says something — there's a power dynamic in the room that's not being resolved."*

15. **How might we make thinking visible through a half-formed idea externalizer** so that participants who are not yet ready to articulate a complete thought can still get an incomplete concept into the room where others can react to and develop it?
*Grounded in Kevin's technique of "throwing stuff at the wall" to surface aha moments: "wait, what did you just say there? Let's unravel that."*

16. **How might we make their thinking visible through warmup artifacts before sessions officially begin** so that first-time participants can signal their mental and emotional state and facilitators can adjust the session design to meet the actual room?
*Grounded in Preston's account of warm faces and physical orientation as prerequisites for psychological readiness, and Bianca's relief when ambiguity was removed before sessions started.*

17. **How might we make thinking visible by surfacing each participant's definition of a contested term before it is used in a decision** so that workshop teams can detect language divergence the moment it emerges rather than discovering it after a direction has already been assumed?
*Grounded in Bianca's verbatim account — "uncovering that in the workshop was critical to actually being able to move forward" — the highest-signal theme across 8 of 9 participants.*

18. **How might we make our thinking visible by creating a persistent shared artifact that evolves throughout the session** so that returning participants in a multi-day workshop can reconstruct what the group concluded yesterday without having to rely on memory or notes taken by one person?
*Grounded in Brandon's echoing-as-conversational-memory technique and the pain point that insight captured at the end often loses the context of when and why it emerged.*

19. **How might we make thinking visible through a structured assumption-surfacing exercise at key decision points** so that senior stakeholders in a workshop can examine the beliefs driving their position before those beliefs become the unspoken premise of a group direction?
*Grounded in Theme 7's anatomy of aha moments — "someone named something that had been implicit" — and Bianca's pivot moment when a bias was verbalized: "Oh, then we can't proceed."*

20. **How might we make collective thinking visible by using a layered synthesis structure — individual to dyad to quad to full group** so that every participant's reasoning is visible at each layer before it is combined, preventing any single voice from collapsing the group's diversity of thought prematurely?
*Grounded in Brandon's description of 1-2-4-All as creating "a different type of coherence" versus extraction, explicitly designed so that insider knowledge compounds rather than the loudest input absorbing the rest.*
