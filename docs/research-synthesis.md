# Research Synthesis: Rethinking Human Communication in Workshops

**Problem Space:** How might we rethink how humans communicate in a workshop?
**Research Method:** Qualitative interviews
**Participants:** 9 individuals
**Date:** March 14, 2026

---

## 2. Key Themes

### Theme 1: Shared Language Is Assumed, Not Established

The most pervasive communication breakdown participants described was using the same word while meaning entirely different things — and not catching it until friction had already accumulated. This happened with abstract terms ("alignment," "system," "celebrity") and technical jargon equally.

> "We use the term a lot, like alignment, like people were throwing around this term alignment. But we, when we started digging into it, we started realizing that what some people meant by alignment — they meant unanimous, like consensus — versus some others felt more like, we're meeting in the middle." — **Bianca**

> "Just even level setting, like, when we say words that mean something in service design, this is what we're referring to... I actually helped start the service design department, and we had to do a lot of this level setting. What do we mean when we say x?" — **Amber**

> "The use of acronyms kills conversations... even when I'm talking to internal folks at Marriott who might know what a CRM is, I might still say the customer relationship management platform... I know other people are having that internal dialog." — **Amber**

> "I've been many meetings when people are like, yeah, they know there's a problem, but it's much harder to resolve." — **Sergio**

The discovery that language had diverged often came too late — after a decision had already been framed or a direction assumed. When it was caught early, it unlocked forward motion. When it wasn't, it caused invisible misalignment that participants only recognized in retrospect.

---

### Theme 2: Visuals Unlock What Words Cannot

Multiple participants independently arrived at the same solution when describing how to convey complex ideas: get it out of your head and onto a surface. Sketches, process maps, journey maps, sticky notes — any artifact that makes thinking visible helps a group achieve shared understanding faster than verbal explanation alone.

> "Visuals work really well. When you have visuals to show folks, it helps a lot... when people can kind of look at it and absorb it at their own pace, without having to hear words that can be misinterpreted." — **Kevin**

> "Visualizing in a way that is like boxes and arrows might be as simple as that... people agree or disagree on it. But visualizing the thinking is what, for me, at least is successful." — **Sergio**

> "You could have a chat, so there are conversations happening and people have avenues to communicate... letting them draw, express." — **Sergio**

> "Storytelling works very well because it takes out ambiguity. Visuals also help, because it doesn't matter how a person learns — it's 100% correlate to how a person communicates." — **Preston**

Participants noted a caveat: visuals are not universally interpreted the same way either. A drawing of a box with an X can be read differently by different people. The power of visualization is in starting the conversation — making the gap visible — not in eliminating ambiguity entirely.

> "You can draw something, and somebody says: that's a car, no, that's a boat, no, that's a plane... When it's in written form, at least the interpretation is explicit." — **Sergio**

### Theme 3: The Loudest Voice Shapes the Room — Unless Deliberately Interrupted

Dominance dynamics were a recurring structural problem. In the absence of deliberate facilitation, the first voice or the highest-status voice tends to set the direction — and quieter participants withdraw, leaving the group poorer for it.

> "Equalizing the voices in the room... something that has been successful is the silent moments that start, because you don't allow the first voices to dominate the room." — **Sergio**

> "We purposely try to do quiet brainstorming — instead of just talking, let's write them down on sticky notes. What that does is allow people to generate their ideas and think thoughtfully, instead of the loudest person in the room taking over." — **Kevin**

> "People who really make the jam about them... it totally derails the design work." — **Yosef**

> "If we only let the loud voices, then the alignment didn't really happen. It just kind of replicated whatever the two people in the room that dominated the conversation were saying." — **Sergio**

Power dynamics compounded this. When a VP or senior stakeholder spoke, others often aligned — not because they agreed, but because of hierarchy. Sergio described this as false consensus: the group checks the box and walks away thinking they've agreed, when in fact nothing has been resolved.

> "You might take something as an assertion — everybody's agreeing — but they're agreeing because the VP says something. There's a power dynamic in the room that's not being resolved." — **Sergio**

### Theme 4: Psychological Safety Is the Foundation — and It's Fragile

Participants consistently linked their willingness to speak up, challenge a direction, or share a half-formed idea to whether the environment felt safe. This safety was established (or undermined) by both explicit signals (stated norms) and implicit ones (how the group responded to the first person who took a risk).

> "Especially in more hierarchical organizations, it becomes more difficult to have good workshop sessions. There is that hesitation to speak up." — **Amber**

> "There are fears... What if what I say is interpreted as: I don't know the topic? There are a lot of more intrinsic barriers to communication that are probably the most challenging, because they require self-awareness." — **Sergio**

> "It was trust. We trust that everyone has an engaging thought. It's just a matter of, can you do it in a way that doesn't offend anybody else?" — **Preston**

> "If I'm transparent as possible — hey, this is my opinion, take it or leave it — at least I said it. I'd rather be honest with you because hopefully I've gained future trust by gaining trust." — **Jeffery**

Sergio also surfaced a more subtle layer: implicit group norms that form despite (or in contradiction to) stated ground rules. A Slack thread that mocks someone's idea after the meeting is an implicit norm that silences voices in the next meeting.

> "Somebody said something, and all of the sudden there's a Slack thread making fun of that person... Even if we say, please speak up, there's an implicit incentive not to." — **Sergio**

---

### Theme 5: Participants Arrive With Different Needs, and Onboarding Rarely Accounts for This

The first 30 minutes of a workshop do enormous work — setting expectations, forming social contracts, orienting people to purpose. But participants arrive at very different starting points: first-timers need scaffolding; experienced jammers want to move fast; some come to learn, others to network, others to ship something.

> "Different people come to the jam for different reasons. Even I come to the jam for different reasons as my craft has evolved. The first time I jammed I wanted to learn and flex those muscles. Later it became more about the fun, the socializing and networking." — **Yosef**

> "Not everybody was starting with the same parameters... Not everyone was onboarding at the same starting line." — **Brandon**

> "The context of the jam needs to be understood before going into sessions — especially for people that are new." — **Preston**

> "It makes me feel more secure — I'm not in a state of ambiguity anymore. It quells all the questions I'm potentially going to have that would cause anxiety or stress." — **Bianca**

Warm physical onboarding details mattered more than expected for newer participants. Preston specifically named "warm faces at the door" and knowing where basic amenities were as preconditions for being comfortable enough to then open up to strangers.

> "Warm faces to greet you... that allows me to be more comfortable, to be more inviting when someone else is also being invited." — **Preston**

### Theme 6: Bridging Techniques — When They Work and When They Don't

Participants who had experience facilitating or participating in structured workshops named a consistent toolkit of bridging techniques: echoing, mirroring, asking clarifying questions, reframing in analogies, and checking for understanding. These were most effective when used proactively, not just as rescue moves after breakdown.

> "We use echoing — I heard you say, or what you said made me think of. You're acknowledging what was said. Mirroring is more detail: here are the specific things I heard you say. It allows you to trace the conversational memory." — **Brandon**

> "I tried to boil it down to something that was simple to understand. It feels like we're trying to build an elevator, but we're building the elevator at the same time they're building the floors, and people are moving into those floors." — **Jeffery**

> "You always need to meet the person where they are... If someone asks what service design is, the first thing I say is: tell me what do you do. And then I try to find something that they do that is a service." — **Yosef**

> "Complexity is hard. You have to simplify. You have to reduce. You have to focus attention on specific things. And then you connect the dots between those smaller parts and say: if you zoom out, this is the bigger picture." — **Brandon**

Analogies were called out specifically as a high-leverage tool — they work when they meet people where they already live, and fail when the analogy itself requires decoding.

> "It's almost similar to how people used religion to convey what's actually happening — I don't understand why the sun comes up, clearly there's some entity. So that's how people explained it before we had the science." — **Jeffery**

---

### Theme 7: The Aha Moment — How Direction Shifts in a Group

Across interviews, participants described specific pivot moments when a group's direction fundamentally changed. These moments shared a common anatomy: someone named something that had been implicit, or introduced an artifact that made the invisible visible, or surfaced an assumption that everyone had held but no one had examined.

> "It was the moment someone verbalized what their bias of alignment meant to them and said it out loud: 'Oh, then we can't proceed — we haven't aligned yet.' And someone else said, 'Oh, wait, that's not how I was thinking about it at all.'" — **Bianca**

> "I noticed that there was untapped potential, unexplored... I had a conversation with the key decision maker and just said: give me two weeks to explore this and show you what's possible. You can tell me whether it's worth the investment." — **Bianca**

> "In track, if you're running a relay, it's very simple to hand off a baton. I was just like: there's nobody to hand a baton off to in this situation. And that was the aha moment." — **Jeffery**

> "Sometimes the best thing to do is just start throwing anything out, throwing stuff at the wall and seeing what sticks. And then there's these aha moments: wait, what did you just say there? Let's unravel that." — **Kevin**

These pivots were not accidental — they were enabled by someone willing to speak up despite the risk, often because they had built enough trust with the group or had enough confidence that the alternative (staying silent) was worse.

## 4. Pain Points

### P1: Language divergence goes undetected until it's costly
Participants regularly described using the same words while meaning fundamentally different things. The breakdown was almost never caught in real time — it surfaced when a decision got stuck, or after someone said "we're not aligned" and others realized they didn't even agree on what alignment meant. By then, time and trust had already eroded.

> "We were using the term alignment... when we started digging in, we realized people had radically different thoughts as to what we mean by alignment. And uncovering that in the workshop was critical to actually being able to move forward." — **Bianca**

### P2: Quiet voices are systematically underweighted
Without deliberate structure, the group's output reflects the most vocal participants — not the most thoughtful ones. Introverts, non-native speakers, junior participants, and newcomers all reported self-silencing. The group loses their perspective and often doesn't know it.

> "If we only let the loud voices, then the alignment didn't really happen. We're just replicating whatever the two people in the room that dominated the conversation were saying." — **Sergio**

> "There are intrinsic barriers to communication — fears about how you'll be perceived — that require self-awareness. They're the most challenging to see." — **Sergio**

### P3: Assumed shared context among mixed-experience groups
Workshops regularly mix first-timers with veterans, domain experts with generalists, and people who came to learn with people who came to network. Onboarding rarely accounts for this range. The result is that scaffolding feels condescending to experienced participants and insufficient for new ones simultaneously.

> "Not everybody was starting with the same parameters." — **Brandon**

> "The first time you jam to the fifth time you jam, it's gonna be totally different." — **Yosef**

### P4: Psychological safety is often assumed, not created
Facilitators may state ground rules, but the implicit norms of a group (how the first risk-taker is treated, whether the hierarchy is acknowledged) determine whether anyone actually follows them. Hierarchical dynamics are especially destructive in workshop settings where psychological safety is a prerequisite for honest input.

> "Especially in more hierarchical organizations, it becomes more difficult to have good sessions... there is that hesitation to speak up." — **Amber**

> "Even if we say, please speak up, there's an implicit incentive not to." — **Sergio**

### P5: Cognitive overload depletes collaborative capacity
Participants arrive at workshops carrying the weight of their week — tired, distracted, or not ready to engage at the level the session demands. Facilitators plan for participants at full capacity; the actual room operates at partial capacity.

> "A lot of times people show up to events after a long week. They're cognitively overloaded. This is a weekend — I don't want to be thinking too hard... we're dealing with people at partial capacity. So how do you be generous and gentle in that regard?" — **Brandon**

### P6: Aha moments and live insights are lost before they're captured
Valuable realizations and unexpected connections happen throughout a workshop, not just at designated reflection points. But the infrastructure for capturing emergent insight in real time is missing. Ideas that aren't captured evaporate.

> "If I'm told to wait until the end of the event, I've probably forgotten a lot of things in the moment... the idea dies as soon as you leave it. So why don't you capture it as it's happening?" — **Brandon**

### P7: False consensus — the check-box alignment problem
Groups routinely leave workshops believing they've aligned when they haven't. The surface agreement is mistaken for real shared understanding. Nobody calls it out because it would require admitting uncertainty or challenging authority.

> "We check the box, yeah. We walk away thinking we agree, and nobody has really agreed. Then you're in swirl." — **Sergio**

## 5. Bright Spots

### What is working well

**Silent parallel ideation (sticky notes, quiet brainstorming)**
Giving everyone simultaneous, silent input time before verbal discussion was called out repeatedly as one of the most effective equalizers. It reduces first-mover bias and creates a visual artifact the whole group can react to.

> "We purposely try to do quiet brainstorming — instead of just talking, it was: let's write them down on sticky notes. What that does is allow people to generate their ideas and think thoughtfully." — **Kevin**

**Echoing and mirroring to maintain conversational coherence**
Brandon's description of echoing ("I heard you say...") and mirroring (detailed reflection) as practices for tracing conversational memory was one of the most concrete and immediately actionable techniques named across all interviews. The "handshake" metaphor — borrowed from networking protocols — resonated as a model for sequenced acknowledgment in human conversation.

> "It allows you to trace the conversational memory. In computers, for a network to work, there's this idea of a handshake — information is passed from one computer to another, acknowledged, then the next message. In conversations, that creates a pause and allows you to reorient." — **Brandon**

**Analogies that meet people where they already live**
When a participant couldn't find technical language that worked, translating the idea into a domain the audience already understood was consistently effective.

> "I tried to boil it down to something simple. It feels like we're trying to build an elevator at the same time the floors are being built and people are moving in." — **Jeffery**

> "When someone asks what service design is, I say: tell me what you do. Then I tie it into that." — **Yosef**

**Explicit level-setting of terms and expectations**
When facilitators took time to define shared vocabulary, explain acronyms, and name what the session was and wasn't trying to accomplish, workshops ran more smoothly. The most experienced practitioners had internalized this as a non-negotiable first step.

> "We'd start a meeting with: let me walk you through what service design is, what the process looks like, what we're talking about, and then go into: okay, now here's what we're doing in this meeting." — **Amber**

**The "pollinator" role — cross-team connectors**
Brandon introduced the concept of a deliberate pollinator: someone whose role is to circulate across isolated working groups, bring questions and insights from one to another, and prevent silos from forming.

> "You need a pollinator — someone who bounces around from each group and pulls the groups together. Constantly making sure we didn't get siloed — build bridges with questions, invite people to give insights." — **Brandon**

**Structured participation formats (1-2-4-All)**
Brandon's description of Liberating Structures — individual reflection, dyad, quad, then full group — as a layered synthesis approach was the most sophisticated group facilitation technique named. It creates coherence through participatory methods, not extraction by a facilitator.

> "Like 1-2-4-All: individual reflection, dyad, quad — each layer they're combining insider knowledge. And then all is: you share it back to the group. That creates a different type of coherence versus someone extracting an insight." — **Brandon**

**Warm, human onboarding basics**
For first-timers especially, small physical hospitality signals (warm greeting, knowing where the bathroom and food are, being oriented before the work starts) were prerequisites for psychological readiness to collaborate.

> "Warm faces to greet you... that allows me to be more comfortable, to be more inviting when someone else is also being invited." — **Preston**

## 6. How Might We Statements

These HMW statements are derived from the themes and pain points above. They are scoped to be actionable but open enough to support a range of ideation directions.

**HMW-1: Shared Language**
How might we help workshop participants surface and align on what key words actually mean before those words do damage?

**HMW-2: Amplifying Quiet Voices**
How might we create structures that give every voice an equal pathway into the conversation — regardless of personality, seniority, or communication style?

**HMW-3: Making Thinking Visible**
How might we make it easier to externalize a half-formed idea so others can react to the concept rather than the words used to describe it?

**HMW-4: Real Versus False Alignment**
How might we help a group distinguish between genuine shared understanding and surface-level compliance — so they know when they've actually aligned versus when they've just moved on?

**HMW-5: Onboarding Across Experience Levels**
How might we design a workshop opening that meets both the first-timer and the seasoned practitioner where they are, without slowing one down or leaving the other behind?

**HMW-6: Capturing Emergent Insight in Real Time**
How might we make it easy for participants to capture and share meaningful moments as they happen — not just at the end of a session?

**HMW-7: Psychological Safety at Scale**
How might we help facilitators surface and respond to implicit group norms — not just the stated ground rules — so that safety is created, not just declared?

**HMW-8: Working With Participants at Their Actual Capacity**
How might we design workshop interactions that are generous to people who are cognitively depleted, distracted, or showing up at partial capacity — without sacrificing the depth of the work?
